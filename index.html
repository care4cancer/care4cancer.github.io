About Cancer

Cancer is a group of diseases involving abnormal cell growth with the potential to invade or spread to other parts of the body. It can affect almost any organ or tissue and arises through multiple factors including genetic mutations, environmental exposure, and lifestyle risks.

Histology & Morphology

Histology refers to the study of the microscopic structure of tissues. In cancer, it helps classify tumors as epithelial, mesenchymal, lymphoid, or germ cell derived. Morphology describes the shape and structure of tumor cells under the microscope, which aids in identifying cancer type and behavior.

Cancer Grading

Grading assesses how much tumor cells differ from healthy cells. Low-grade tumors resemble normal tissue and tend to grow slowly, while high-grade tumors appear abnormal and are often more aggressive. Common grading systems include the Gleason score for prostate cancer and Nottingham score for breast cancer.

Cancer Staging

Staging describes how much cancer is in the body and helps determine treatment options and prognosis. The most widely used system is the TNM classification developed by the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC):

  • T (Tumor): Size and extent of the primary tumor
  • N (Nodes): Involvement of regional lymph nodes
  • M (Metastasis): Spread to distant organs

Stages range from 0 (in situ) to IV (advanced/metastatic). Source: National Cancer Institute

Cancer Staging Based on Tumor Size and Dissemination:
Oncologists utilize a numerical staging system to classify the extent of malignant disease progression. The stages are defined as follows:

Stage 0: Carcinoma in situ — atypical epithelial cells are present but confined to the basement membrane without invasion into adjacent tissues. This stage represents pre-invasive neoplasia and is often curable with local treatment.

Stage I: Localized malignancy — the primary tumor is small in dimension, confined to the organ of origin, and shows no evidence of regional lymphatic or distant metastasis.

Stage II: Locally advanced cancer — the neoplasm has increased in size and/or exhibits limited spread to regional lymph nodes, but without distant metastatic involvement.

Stage III: Regionally advanced disease — the tumor demonstrates extensive local invasion into surrounding tissues and/or has metastasized to multiple or large regional lymph nodes.

Stage IV: Metastatic cancer — malignant cells have disseminated beyond the primary site and regional nodes to distant organs, indicating systemic disease.
